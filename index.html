Human Impact · 20 minutes
The People Behind the Numbers
~20 min

The numbers tell part of the story. But behind each statistic is a family. The Great Recession didn't just happen on Wall Street — it happened in living rooms, parking lots, and kitchen tables across America. Read the accounts below.

Account A — A Homeowner in Cleveland, Ohio (2009)
"We bought the house in 2005. The bank told us we qualified — we were so excited. Two years later the payment jumped because it was an adjustable rate, and we couldn't keep up. We tried everything. By the time the foreclosure notice came, we had three kids. We had 30 days to leave the only home they'd ever known. My husband sat in the car in the driveway for an hour before we drove away."
— Oral history collected by the Federal Reserve Bank of Cleveland, 2009
Account B — A Factory Worker in Detroit, Michigan (2009)
"I worked at that plant for 22 years. When GM went bankrupt, I lost my job, my pension, and my health insurance in the same week. I was 54. Nobody's hiring a 54-year-old machinist. I started applying to Walmart. My wife cried. I cried. You work your whole life thinking that's enough. Turns out it wasn't."
— Interview, Detroit Free Press, June 2009
Foreclosure signs became a defining image of the Great Recession across American suburbs — [photographer/source]

Analysis Questions

1. What specific details from these accounts hit hardest? Why does the human story land differently than the statistics?

2. Who seems to be bearing the most pain in these accounts — the banks, the government, or ordinary people? Is that fair? Explain.

Political Cartoon Analysis · 15 minutes
Who Caused This — and Who Got Blamed?
~15 min

When the crisis hit, the government bailed out the biggest banks and Wall Street firms. But in media and political debates, a very different story was being told about who was really at fault. This cartoon captures that contradiction.

📰 "The Pundits" — Steve Greenberg, 2008
Key details: A suited figure labeled "The Pundits" points at a figure labeled "The Poor" and says "This is all your fault... you bought a house you couldn't really afford!" Surrounding them in the rubble: AIG, Merrill Lynch, Lehman Brothers, GM, Bear Stearns, Washington Mutual — along with labels reading "Toxic Assets," "Executive Bonuses," "Bundled Subprime Mortgage Securities," "Mega-Deficits," and "Layoffs."

Cartoon Analysis

1. Describe what you see. Who are "The Pundits" and "The Poor"? What is happening between them? List at least three things you notice in the rubble around them.

2. Who is being blamed for the crisis in this cartoon? Look carefully at everything in the rubble. Does the evidence actually support that blame? What does the cartoonist think the real causes were?

3. Connect the cartoon to the human accounts in Section 2. Does the Cleveland homeowner or the Detroit factory worker fit the "The Poor" figure being blamed? Is the blame fair? Use evidence from both the cartoon and the accounts.

Historical Significance · 12 minutes
What This Moment Meant
~12 min

On November 4, 2008, Barack Obama was elected the 44th President of the United States — becoming the first African American to hold that office in the nation's 232-year history.

To understand why this was extraordinary, you have to hold two timelines in your head at once: the timeline of American ideals — "all men are created equal" — and the timeline of American reality, which looked very different for Black Americans for most of the country's history.

The arc of history — a quick timeline

1865 13th Amendment abolishes slavery — 89 years after the Declaration of Independence
1896 Supreme Court upholds segregation in Plessy v. Ferguson
1955 Rosa Parks arrested; Montgomery Bus Boycott begins
1963 Martin Luther King Jr. delivers "I Have a Dream" speech
1965 Voting Rights Act passed — 100 years after the end of slavery
2008 Barack Obama elected President — 43 years after the Voting Rights Act
Primary Source — Jesse Jackson at Grant Park, November 4, 2008
Television cameras caught civil rights leader Jesse Jackson — who had marched with Martin Luther King Jr. and stood beside him when he was assassinated in 1968 — weeping openly in the crowd as Obama's victory was announced. He said nothing. He didn't have to.
— CNN Election Night Coverage, November 4, 2008
Jesse Jackson weeping at Grant Park, Chicago, November 4, 2008 — CNN Election Night Coverage

Reflection Questions

1. Look at the arc of history timeline. What does it tell you about how long change takes in America? Does Obama's election feel like progress, completion, or something else?

2. Jesse Jackson said nothing and didn't need to. What do you think he was feeling? What memories might have been going through his mind in that moment?

Political Cartoon Analysis · 12 minutes
Standing on the Shoulders of Giants
~12 min

Matt Wuerker, a cartoonist for Politico, drew this image for Obama's inauguration. He later said that drawing the first Black president required him to think carefully about American history — not just the present moment, but everything that made the moment possible.

📰 "Standing on the Shoulders of Giants" — Matt Wuerker, Politico, January 20, 2009
Artist: Matt Wuerker, Politico  |  Published: January 20, 2009  |  What to notice: Who is at the top? Who is in the middle tier? Who forms the foundation at the bottom? What does the building they're standing on represent?

Cartoon Analysis

1. Describe the structure of the cartoon from bottom to top. Who do you see at each level? What do you think each figure represents? Look for clues in their clothing, poses, and any details you recognize.

2. The cartoon's central idea is that Obama didn't get there alone — he is literally standing on the people who came before him. Who are some of the people or groups from the arc-of-history timeline in Section 4 that you think are represented in this structure? Why?

3. Look at Jesse Jackson's face in the photograph from Section 4. Now look at this cartoon. How do they tell the same story in two completely different ways? What does each one capture that the other can't?

Discussion question: The cartoon suggests that individual achievement is always built on collective sacrifice. Do you agree? Can you think of other examples — in history or in your own life — where someone's success was built on what others did before them?

Primary Source Excerpt — Victory Speech, November 4, 2008
"If there is anyone out there who still doubts that America is a place where all things are possible; who still wonders if the dream of our founders is alive in our time; who still questions the power of our democracy — tonight is your answer... It's been a long time coming, but tonight, because of what we did on this day, in this election, at this defining moment, change has come to America."
— Barack Obama, Grant Park, Chicago, November 4, 2008
Primary Source Excerpt — Inaugural Address, January 20, 2009
"Today I say to you that the challenges we face are real. They are serious and they are many. They will not be met easily or in a short span of time. But know this, America — they will be met... Let us remember that if this financial crisis taught us anything, it's that we cannot have a thriving Wall Street while Main Street suffers."
— Barack Obama, Washington D.C., January 20, 2009

2. In the inaugural address, Obama quotes the financial crisis directly: "Wall Street while Main Street suffers." What does this phrase mean? Why does he use it on inauguration day?
